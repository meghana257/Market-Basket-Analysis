Project Objective

This project analyses global online retail transactions to uncover customer purchasing patterns, identify cross-selling opportunities, and generate data-driven recommendations using Market Basket Analysis. Raw transactional data is transformed through cleaning, feature engineering, exploratory analysis, and association rule mining (Apriori) into actionable business insights.

Dataset Overview

The source is the UCI Online Retail dataset — a real-world transnational transaction log from a UK-based non-store retailer operating between December 2010 and December 2011.

Data Processing

A disciplined preprocessing pipeline transforms raw transaction logs into a clean, analysis-ready dataset. Every step protects the integrity of the basket-level associations derived downstream.

Preprocessing Pipeline

StepActionReason

Descriptive Analytics

What the data shows

The United Kingdom dominates with 17,906 transactions (90.5% of the 19,781 global total) and £8.75M revenue — roughly 85.1% of the £10.28M company-wide top line.

Revenue is highly concentrated: the next-largest market by revenue is the Netherlands at just £283K — 31× smaller than the UK — highlighting steep regional dependency and a long tail of underperforming markets.

Average revenue-per-transaction tells a different story: the Netherlands averages £3,053 per order and Australia £2,466, vastly outperforming the UK's £489 — these are low-volume but high-value B2B-style markets.

Prescriptive Analytics

What the business should do

Prioritise the UK with personalised promotions and a loyalty programme — a 1% lift on the £8.75M UK base equals £87,500 incremental revenue, far more than any single secondary market currently generates.

The 6,802 invoices with 20+ items (34% of all transactions) confirm strong co-purchasing behaviour — cross-sell widgets and "frequently bought together" prompts will compound on the largest basket segment.

Target Netherlands, EIRE and Australia with premium-tier B2B campaigns — their AOV is 5–6× the UK average, so even modest transaction growth here drives outsized revenue lift.

UK Exploratory Analysis

After filtering to the UK, the analysis surfaces hourly demand patterns, monthly seasonality, and transaction-level structure that inform timing and merchandising decisions.

Top 10 Selling Products by Quantity

Descriptive Insight

The top SKU — PAPER CRAFT, LITTLE BIRDIE — sold 80,995 units, narrowly ahead of MEDIUM CERAMIC TOP STORAGE JAR (77,036). The top 10 products together account for ~9.9% of all UK units sold (4.64M).

Prescriptive Insight

Stock these 10 SKUs as never-out-of-stock items and feature them in homepage carousels — their consistent demand makes them the safest anchor products for cross-sell bundles and loyalty rewards.

Basket Size Contribution

Descriptive Insight

The largest segment is 1–5 items (4,045 invoices), followed closely by 21–30 items (2,580). The UK average basket is 26.44 unique products per invoice — well above typical e-commerce baskets.

Prescriptive Insight

Trigger an "add one more" recommendation prompt as baskets approach 20 items — over 6,673 invoices already exceed 30 items, proving customers respond to depth-of-cart nudges.

Hourly Transaction Trend

Descriptive Insight

Transactions peak sharply at 12:00 with 2,936 invoices, with the entire 10:00–15:00 window driving 13,960 invoices (78% of UK volume). Activity is effectively zero before 06:00 and after 20:00.

Prescriptive Insight

Send marketing emails between 09:30 and 10:00 to ride the morning ramp into noon, and concentrate live-chat staffing in the 10:00–15:00 band where conversion opportunity is highest.

Monthly Demand Trend

Descriptive Insight

Demand triples from a low of 229,539 units (Feb 2011) to a peak of 648,000 units (Nov 2011) — a +182% surge. The Sept–Nov ramp consistently outsells every other month.

Prescriptive Insight

Begin Q4 inventory replenishment by early September and lock in supplier capacity by August. Pull forward marketing budget to capture the November peak rather than spreading it evenly.

Business Recommendations

Six data-driven recommendations grounded directly in the UK retail analysis, association-rule mining, seasonal demand patterns, and customer purchasing behaviour uncovered through this project.

01
UK Market Priority

Concentrate personalisation and retention investment in the UK

The UK accounts for 17,906 of 19,781 global transactions and £8.75M of £10.28M total revenue — roughly 85% of company revenue. Personalised loyalty programmes and UK-specific promotions will produce the highest return on marketing spend.

02
Seasonal Stocking

Front-load inventory for the Q4 demand surge from September

UK monthly demand peaks at 648,000 units in November 2011 — a 182% uplift over the lowest month (February, 229,539 units). Replenishment for high-volume SKUs should begin by September to avoid stockouts in the peak window.

03
Peak-Hour Targeting

Schedule promotions and campaigns between 10:00 and 14:00

Hourly UK transactions peak at 12:00 with 2,936 invoices — over 2× the daily average. Flash sales, email send times, and chat staffing should be concentrated in the 10:00–14:00 band to ride the natural demand peak.

04
Herb Garden Cross-Sell

Deploy a "Complete Your Herb Garden" widget on every herb marker page

Herb marker pairings produce the strongest signals in the dataset — 1→1 rules with lift 70–72 and confidence 0.92, and 1→2 rules with lift 76 and confidence 0.87. A "Complete the Set" widget on every herb marker page is the single highest-ROI recommendation action available.

05
Seasonal Bundle Kits

Launch wooden Christmas and Regency tea-set bundles for Q4

Two non-herb bundles emerge with extreme lift: Heart + Star wooden Christmas decorations (lift 60.89) and Regency Milk Jug Pink + Sugar Bowl Green (lift 50.85). Both should be packaged as gift kits for the Q4 peak window with a small multi-buy discount.

06
Basket Growth Prompt

Trigger "add one more" prompts as baskets approach 20 items

The average UK basket size is 26.44 unique products per transaction, with 2,580 invoices in the 21–30 range. A dynamic cart prompt powered by association rules — fired once a basket reaches 20 items — will systematically push customers toward and beyond the behavioural average.
